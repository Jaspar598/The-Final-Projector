Movement

This chapter will focus on five of the 12 principles of animation: Follow through and overlapping action, Slow In and Slow Out, Arcs, Secondary Action, and Timing.

These principles are essential for creating smooth and natural movement in animation.

Follow Through and Overlapping Action

This principle states that different parts of an animated figure will continue to move after the main part or center of gravity has stopped. For example, if a character is running and suddenly stops, their hair should swing forward and continue its motion before stopping.

Slow In and Slow Out

This principle states that movements shoulds start and end slowly by adding more frames near the beginning and end of a movement. This creates a more natural and realistic motion that emphasizes the acceleration and deceleration of objects.

Arcs

This principle simply states that most natural movements follow an arc or circular path instead of rigid motions, and thus, actions should be animated as such.

Secondary Action

This principle states that additional actions should be added to compliment a main action. This adds more dimension to the character animation and helps to support the main action. For example, a character walking might swing their arms or have their clothes move slightly with each step.

Timing

This principle refers to the number of frames used for a given action, which affects the speed and rhythm of the movement. Proper timing can convey weight, emotion, and personality in animated characters.
