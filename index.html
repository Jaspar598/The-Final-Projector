12 Principles of Animation

The 12 Principles of Animation were developed by Disney animators Ollie Johnston and Frank Thomas in their 1981 book "The Illusion of Life: Disney Animation." These principles are still used by animators today. The principles names are as follows:

  1. Squash and Stretch
  2. Anticipation
  3. Staging
  4. Straight Ahead and Pose to Pose
  5. Follow Through and Overlapping Action
  6. Slow In and Slow Out
  7. Arcs
  8. Secondary Action
  9. Timing
  10. Exaggeration
  11. Solid Drawing
  12. Appeal

Each principle is designed to enhance the believability and emotional impact of animated characters and objects. By applying these principles, animators can create more dynamic and engaging animations that capture audiences' attention and convey a sense of life and personality. Here are brief, practical examples and tips for each principle:

Squash and Stretch — Emphasize volume and flexibility to show weight and impact (e.g., a bouncing ball squashes on contact and stretches as it lifts).

Anticipation — Add a small preparatory move before a big action (a character crouches before jumping) to make motion readable.

Staging — Use clear poses, lighting, and composition so the audience immediately understands the action and focus.

Straight Ahead and Pose to Pose — Straight ahead animation creates fluid spontaneity; pose-to-pose builds strong key poses and controlled timing. Combine both for balance.

Follow Through and Overlapping Action — Let parts of a character continue moving after the main action stops (hair, clothing, tails) to avoid rigid stops.

Slow In and Slow Out — Add more frames at the start and end of an action to create believable acceleration and deceleration.

Arcs — Most natural motion follows curved paths; use arcs to make movement feel organic and graceful.

Secondary Action — Layer small actions (a sigh, a hand gesture) that support the main motion without distracting from it.

Timing — Control the number of frames and spacing to convey weight, mood, and personality; faster for light/energetic, slower for heavy/solemn.

Exaggeration — Push poses and expressions beyond realism to strengthen clarity and emotional impact while keeping believability.

Solid Drawing — Maintain consistent volume, weight, and anatomy in drawings so forms feel three-dimensional and believable.

Appeal — Design characters and poses with clear silhouettes, readable expressions, and a distinct personality that the audience connects with. Practice tip: study classic animation, break scenes into key poses, and repeat short exercises (bouncing ball, walk cycles, simple acting shots). Combine principles deliberately, test variations in timing and spacing, and iterate until the motion reads clearly and emotionally.
