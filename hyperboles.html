Hyperboles

This chapter will focus on three of the 12 principles of animation, Squash and Stretch, Anticipation,
and Exaggeration.

These principles are what truly brings animation to life because of the way that they exaggerate motion in a way that is appealing to the viewer. Without these three principles, animations would feel stiff and lifeless.

Squash and Stretch

Squash and Stretch is the principle that gives a sense of weight and flexibility to objects. It is used to show the impact of forces on an object, such as gravity or momentum. For example, when a ball bounces, it squashes when it hits the ground and stretches as it lifts off. This principle can also be used to exaggerate facial expressions and body movements, making characters more dynamic and expressive.

Anticipation

Anticipation is the principle that prepares the audience for an action that is about to happen. It is used to create a sense of expectation and build tension before a movement occurs. For example, before a character jumps, they may crouch down slightly to prepare for the leap. This principle helps to make movements feel more natural and believable, enhancing the overall impact of the animation.

Exaggeration

Exaggeration is the principle that amplifies movements and expressions to make them more visually interesting and engaging. It is used to emphasize key actions and emotions, making them more memorable for the audience. For example, a character's reaction to a surprise might be exaggerated with wide eyes and an open mouth to convey shock more effectively. This principle helps to create a stronger connection between the audience and the characters, enhancing the overall impact of the animation.
